Base-i vs Mod-N vs FFT: Technical Comparison

Core Differences

  • FFT is a global transform that reveals frequency content but loses time-order and phase continuity.
  • Base-i is a per-sample complex encoding that preserves structure, phase, and temporal sequence.
  • Mod-N generalizes Base-i using modular arithmetic across cyclic roots, enabling tailored encodings with strong reconstruction performance.

Summary

FFT is powerful for direct frequency decomposition and fast convolution but is limited in temporal resolution, structure retention, and sample-level fidelity.

Base-i and Mod-N preserve exact time-domain geometry, enabling lossless storage, real-time decoding, and signal structure analysis. Mod-N, in particular, allows tuning of the encoding system using modular roots (e.g., Mod-12), offering phase-aware, compact representations with zero reconstruction error under optimal configurations.










In applications requiring exact time-domain reconstruction and preservation of signal integrity, Base-i and Mod-N methods demonstrate superiority over traditional FFT-based approaches by achieving zero mean squared error (MSE), zero absolute error, and zero residual imaginary components in tested waveforms. FFT, while efficient for frequency-domain analysis, often introduces minor reconstruction artifacts due to its global transform nature, floating-point precision limitations, and potential spectral leakage—resulting in non-zero MSE (e.g., up to 5.31e-33 in our benchmarks) and residual imaginary values (e.g., up to 21.77). These advantages make Base-i and Mod-N particularly suitable for high-precision tasks like lossless audio archiving or noise-resilient data transmission, assuming signal lengths align with the cyclic bases & no frequency decomposition needed.
